About Guruvayoor Temple

The Sacred Abode of Lord Krishna • Dwarka of the South

Guruvayoor Temple is one of the most prominent and sacred Hindu temples in South India, often called the 'Dwarka of the South'. This holy site is a hub of devotion, tradition, and cultural richness that has endured for millennia.

Nestled in the town of Guruvayur, Kerala, this sacred temple is dedicated to Lord Krishna, revered here as 'Unnikrishnan' - the divine child form of Krishna. The temple holds an esteemed place in the hearts of millions of devotees who flock here seeking blessings and spiritual solace.

The presiding deity is believed to be over 5,000 years old and was once worshipped by Lord Krishna Himself in Dwaraka. Legend has it that after the submergence of Dwaraka, the idol was brought to Kerala by Guru (the preceptor of the gods) and Vayu (the wind god), giving the temple its name - Guruvayoor (Guru+Vayu+Oor).

Sacred Highlights
The main deity is made of a rare stone called Patala Anjanam and portrays Krishna in a standing posture with four arms (Chathurbahu), radiating divine grace.
Temple rituals are conducted in accordance with centuries-old Tantric and Vedic traditions, preserving ancient worship methods unchanged for generations.
Thousands of devotees perform sacred offerings like Thulabharam (weighing oneself against offerings), Archana, and special poojas daily.
Guruvayoor Ekadasi and Janmashtami are celebrated with grand spiritual and cultural events that attract devotees from across the world.
Elephants are an integral part of temple culture. Punnathur Kotta, the elephant sanctuary, houses the temple's majestic elephants in a unique tradition.
More than just a religious center, Guruvayoor represents Kerala's spiritual identity and cultural heritage, embodying timeless traditions.

The temple's architecture follows the traditional Kerala style, with its golden flagstaff (Kodimaram) and exquisite murals depicting scenes from Krishna's life. The sanctum sanctorum, with its copper-plated roof and gold-plated flag post, radiates divine energy that has drawn devotees for centuries.

What makes Guruvayoor unique is its strict adherence to traditions. The temple maintains ancient customs like the Nirmalya Darshan (early morning viewing of the deity adorned with previous day's flowers) and Seeveli (procession of elephants and percussion ensembles) that create a living connection to India's spiritual past.

Visitors are humbly requested to follow the traditional dress code (men: dhoti; women: sari/mundum neriyathum) and maintain the sanctity of the temple premises. Photography inside the sanctum is strictly prohibited.
